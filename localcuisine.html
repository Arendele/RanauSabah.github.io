Local Cuisine

—in Ranau,Sabah—

HINAVA

Hinava is a traditional dish from the Malaysian state of Sabah, specifically associated with the Kadazan-Dusun ethnic group. It is a simple and flavorful seafood salad made from fresh raw fish, typically mackerel or pomfret, which is marinated in lime juice. The acidity of the lime juice "cooks" the fish, giving it a slightly firm texture and a tangy taste. The marinated fish is then mixed with sliced onions, chili peppers, ginger, and sometimes grated coconut. The combination of these ingredients creates a refreshing and zesty flavor profile. Hinava is often served as an appetizer or side dish and is popular during festive occasions and cultural celebrations in Sabah. Its simplicity and use of fresh ingredients make it a beloved and cherished dish among locals and visitors alike

Ambuyat

It is made from the sago palm tree, specifically the starch extracted from the trunk of the tree. The starch is mixed with water and cooked until it forms a thick, sticky porridge-like consistency. Ambuyat is typically served with a dipping sauce made from ingredients such as chili, lime juice, and belacan (shrimp paste). To eat ambuyat, a special utensil called a candas is used to twirl the sticky starch around and then dip it into the sauce before consuming. It is a popular dish in Sabah and is enjoyed for its unique texture and the fun and interactive way it is eaten.

Ngiu Chap

It is a flavorful beef noodle soup that combines different cuts of beef with noodles and a rich broth. The dish typically includes tender slices of beef, beef balls, and beef tripe, along with vegetables such as bok choy and bean sprouts. The noodles used can vary, but it is commonly served with yellow egg noodles or rice vermicelli. The broth is made by simmering beef bones and spices for hours, resulting in a savory and aromatic base. Ngiu Chap is often garnished with fried garlic, spring onions, and sometimes chili sauce for an extra kick of flavor. It is a comforting and hearty dish enjoyed by locals and visitors alike in Sabah.

TUARAN MEE

The dish typically consists of yellow noodles that are stir-fried with a variety of ingredients, including slices of char siu (barbecued pork), vegetables such as cabbage and bean sprouts, and sometimes prawns or chicken. The noodles are seasoned with a flavorful mixture of soy sauce, oyster sauce, and other condiments, giving them a savory and slightly sweet taste. Tuaran Mee is often garnished with spring onions and served with a side of pickled green chilies. The dish is known for its distinct texture, with the noodles being springy and slightly chewy. It has gained popularity beyond Tuaran and can now be found in many eateries across Sabah, delighting locals and visitors alike with its unique flavors and satisfying combination of ingredients.

TUHAU

Tuhau dish in Ranau, Sabah, refers to a specific way of preparing and serving tuhau, a traditional food condiment in Sabah, Malaysia. In Ranau, tuhau is often enjoyed as a standalone dish or as a side dish accompanying other main dishes. The dish typically consists of fresh tuhau shoots that have been thinly sliced and mixed with various ingredients. These ingredients may include lime or calamansi juice, bird's eye chili peppers, shallots, salt, and sometimes a touch of sugar. The mixture is then left to marinate for a short period, allowing the flavors to blend together. The resulting tuhau dish is flavorful, with a combination of tangy, spicy, and slightly sour tastes. It is commonly served as an appetizer or a condiment to complement meals, adding a unique and distinctive flavor to the local cuisine of Ranau, Sabah.

LINOPOT

It is a flavorful and hearty meal that consists of layers of rice, meat, and various ingredients, all cooked together in a bamboo tube. To prepare linopot, the bamboo tube is first lined with banana leaves to prevent the ingredients from sticking. Then, a layer of rice is added followed by a layer of marinated meat, usually chicken or beef. Other ingredients such as sliced vegetables, herbs, and spices are added to enhance the flavors. The bamboo tube is then sealed with banana leaves and cooked over an open fire or charcoal, allowing the steam to cook the ingredients inside. This cooking method imparts a distinct smoky aroma and infuses the dish with flavors from the bamboo. Once cooked, the linopot is carefully opened, revealing the layers of rice and tender meat. It is typically served hot and enjoyed as a communal meal, with everyone digging into the bamboo tube to savor the delicious combination of flavors and textures. Linopot is not only a tasty dish but also a symbol of the traditional culinary heritage of Ranau, reflecting the harmony between nature and the local community's culinary creativity.
